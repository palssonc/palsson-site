Research

Institutions, history, and development over the long run

My work studies how institutions such as property rights and state capacity evolve in developing countries. Much of it examines Haiti’s economic history, while another branch looks at how newer technologies and platforms affect everyday outcomes.

View full CV Market Power

Core research themes

I am especially interested in how weak institutions persist, how historical shocks leave durable imprints, and how governance shapes market outcomes.

Regional emphasis

Haiti sits at the center of much of this work, both because its history is deeply important and because it offers unusually revealing evidence on political economy and development.

Complementary topics

I also study transportation and technology adoption, including ride-hailing, public transit, and the unintended effects of smartphones.

Early Experiments in Soft Power: State-Building in Haiti, 1915-34

with Christian Fignole

Uses intelligence reports from the U.S. occupation of Haiti to study whether early state-building efforts persuaded Haitian elites to support the occupation.

with Parker Sonnenberg

Selected publications

Representative published work

Narrow Paths Out of Poverty and Educational Demand: Evidence from Dominican Baseball

with Brian Marein

Studies whether professional baseball recruiting in the Dominican Republic reduced educational attainment.

VancUber: The Effect of Ride-Hailing on Public Transportation, Congestion, and Traffic Fatalities

with John Cairncross and Jonathan Hall

Uses Vancouver’s long Uber ban to identify the long-run effects of ride-hailing on transit, congestion, and traffic fatalities.

The Inefficacy of Land Titling Programs: Homesteading in Haiti, 1933-1950

Shows how a formal titling program in Haiti failed to gain traction because the process was too burdensome for many households.

“A Whirligig of Revolutionary Presidents”: State Capacity, Political Stability, and Business in Haiti, 1905-1927

Links the end of political instability during the American occupation to shifts in state capacity and business activity.

The Forces of Path Dependence: Haiti’s Refugee Camps, 1937-2009

Explores why refugee settlements persisted for generations, emphasizing access to land and social networks rather than simple immobility.

State Capacity, Property Rights, and External Revenues: Haiti, 1932-1949

Shows how wartime revenue gains in Haiti strengthened fiscal and legal capacity.

The Medium-Run Effects of a Foreign Election Intervention: Haiti’s Presidential Elections, 2010-2015

Examines how outside intervention after the earthquake affected voter participation and electoral politics.

Small Farms, Large Transaction Costs: Haiti’s Missing Sugar

Connects property-rights frictions to Haiti’s failure to redevelop a major sugar industry in the early twentieth century.

Is Uber a Substitute or Complement to Public Transit?

with Jonathan Hall and Joseph Price

Finds that Uber often complements public transit, though the relationship varies across cities.

Smartphones and Child Injuries

Links the expansion of smartphone access to increases in injuries among young children, consistent with caregiver distraction.
